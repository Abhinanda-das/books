2 The Problem You know the problem... Even in your bedroom, your home studio, or a small project studio you can have great tools at your disposal - a top class mic preamp as well as excellent compression and EQs – but, despite the quality of the performances, the vocalist's best take is marred by noise and room tone. You can try to sort these things out using denoisers and EQ, but why not use a dedicated pre-processor designed specifically to reduce or even eliminate the problem? 

## The solution… 

… is C-Vox. Designed as a pre-processor that sits between your mic preamp and whatever follows, this has two modes to clean up your vocals and give your mix the best possible chance to shine. 

**Noise mode** has been designed to reduce general background noise and other problems encountered in small studios such as computer noise, allowing you to dial-in the amount of attenuation and choose how the process handles genuine ambience in the signal. 

**Room mode** is optimised to eliminate room tone (the nasty resonances that make your finest performances sound 'honky') making it possible to achieve professional sounding results in small spaces.

## **Noise/Room** 

Switch between two modes of operation. 

Choose **Noise** mode to reduce or eliminate general broadband background noise. All frequency ranges are treated equally in this mode. 

**Room** mode concentrates the processing upon prominent elements of the noise so that it reduces or eliminates room tones (resonances and reverberation). This mode is more selective in the low and mid frequencies in which room tone tends to lurk. 

## **Attenuation** 

This adjusts the amount of noise or room tone attenuation applied to the signal. 

At **low values** , little or no attenuation occurs. 

At **high values** , the noise or room tone is highly attenuated. In general, you should find the minimum amount of Attenuation that achieves the desired result. 

## **Ambience** 

This determines how C-Vox responds to genuine ambience in the incoming signal. 

At **low values** , the process creates a drier output. Be careful with this, because over-processing may result in an output that sounds gated. 

At **high values** , this allows more genuine ambience to pass. But again be careful; too high a value may make it difficult to achieve the desired attenuation of the noise or the room tone. 

## **Bypass** 

Use this to bypass C-Vox’s processing and make before/after comparisons.

4 The Display 

Although the vertical (Y) axis displays signal amplitude and the horizontal (X) axis displays frequency, the C-Vox display is unconventional and it may not be immediately apparent what it represents. 

When processing is bypassed       the display will be shown in grey. 

## Above the zero line (blue areas) 

Areas of the display above the zero line at any given moment show signal and natural ambience that, to a large degree, are unaffected by noise or room tone. The following display illustrates the power distribution of a signal that, at that moment, requires no noise/room reduction.

## History 

The display shows a short history of the changes in the audio signal. The fainter the representation, the further in the past that it lies. The history is displayed for around 0.5s before the current moment; beyond this point, its intensity drops below 5% of the ‘current’ intensity. The following display illustrates a signal that amongst other changes has (i) recently undergone significant attenuation at low frequencies but which now has a strong wanted signal at those frequencies, and (ii) has a falling high frequency content shown by a lower current level than that of the recent past. 


![](<C-Suite C-Vox Manual_assets/C-Suite_C-Vox_Manual.pdf-0011-03.png>)


The following image shows an interesting case. The white line is above the zero line at all frequencies, which means that all of the noise reduction visible below the zero line is history. This suggests that, at the moment that this image was grabbed, the signal had just moved from a period of silence (just noise present) to a period of strong signal. 


![](<C-Suite C-Vox Manual_assets/C-Suite_C-Vox_Manual.pdf-0011-05.png>)


Furthermore, you can see that there is strong signal in the low and mid frequencies that is also in the recent history, which suggest that there was a transient of some nature when the wanted signal started. Consequently, this image indicates that the signal went from just noise to a loud initial signal before settling to its current level. 

There’s a lot that you can observe in the display once you learn to interpret what it’s telling you.

5 Background Noise Reduction<br>**----- End of picture text -----**<br>


This image shows a consistent amount of noise across all frequencies. 


![](<C-Suite C-Vox Manual_assets/C-Suite_C-Vox_Manual.pdf-0012-03.png>)


To reduce the noise, select the Noise mode followed by suitable values for the Attenuation and Ambience. For many signals, a good starting point is Atten = 40 and Ambience = 30. 

Adjust the two parameters to obtain the desired result. Increasing the Atten value will remove more noise from the signal. Reducing the Ambience will make the output sound drier. Likewise, decreasing the Attenuation and increasing the Ambience will reduce the amount of noise reduction and allow the signal to sound more ‘open’.

6 Reducing Room Tone<br>**----- End of picture text -----**<br>


Here you can see clear troughs in the signal that represent a room tone or a cluster of such tones that colour the signal. 


![](<C-Suite C-Vox Manual_assets/C-Suite_C-Vox_Manual.pdf-0013-03.png>)


To reduce or eliminate these, select the Room mode followed by suitable values for the Attenuation and Ambience. A good starting point for these is Atten = 50 and Ambience = 25. Again, you should then adjust both parameters to achieve the most appropriate amount of reduction without making the signal sound too dry (unless that is what is wanted) and without using extreme settings that might introduce unwanted artefacts. 

As before, increasing the Atten value will remove more room tone from the signal and reducing the Ambience will make the output sound drier. Likewise, decreasing the Attenuation and increasing the Ambience will reduce the amount of tone removal and allow the signal to sound more ‘open’.
